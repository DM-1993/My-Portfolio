My Journey

My product journey started at Deloitte, where I got thrown into the deep end building risk management tools for massive banks and pharma companies. Nothing teaches you about enterprise complexity like trying to automate compliance processes that span multiple countries and regulatory frameworks. It was here I learned that the fanciest technical solution means nothing if people won't actually use it.

This lesson became crystal clear at Reckitt, where I took on three regulatory platforms that were... let's say "challenging." Picture thousands of products, 30+ countries, legacy systems that barely talked to each other, and teams skeptical of yet another "digital transformation." The cloud migration to AWS was the easy part—getting people to embrace new workflows? That took everything I learned about change management, user research, and lots of coffee conversations.

Now at Airtel, I'm working on something that really excites me: a workforce platform that actually makes people's jobs easier. We're using AI not because it's trendy, but because it can eliminate the boring, repetitive stuff so people can focus on what matters. When I see our onboarding time drop from 4 weeks to 5 days, I know we're solving real problems.

October 2024

Why AI Product Strategy Should Start with Humans, Not Models

After building multiple AI-powered products, I've learned that the best AI features are invisible to users. They just work. Here's my framework for AI-first product development:

The Problem with AI-First Thinking

Most teams start with "How can we use LLMs?" instead of "What problem are we solving?" This leads to impressive demos that users abandon after a week.

My 3-Layer Approach

  1. Human Layer: What's the user's actual workflow? Where do they get frustrated?
  2. Intelligence Layer: What decisions can we automate without changing their mental model?
  3. AI Layer: Which models/APIs actually solve this specific use case?

Real Example: In my DM Academy platform, students don't interact with "AI tutoring." They just get help that happens to be incredibly personalized. The AI is the means, not the message.

September 2024

The No-Code Revolution is Actually a PM Evolution

Building no-code platforms taught me more about product management than any framework ever could. Here's why:

The Constraint Paradox

When you can build anything, you build nothing useful. No-code forces you to think in terms of user workflows, not developer capabilities.

What I Learned Building Workflow Builders

  • Users don't want infinite flexibility—they want their specific problem solved elegantly
  • The best abstractions hide complexity without limiting power
  • Documentation is a product smell—if you need it, your UX failed

My no-code platform reduced onboarding from weeks to days not because it was "easier to use," but because it matched how users already think about their work.

August 2024

Product Metrics vs. Product Progress: Why I Stopped Chasing Numbers

After years of obsessing over conversion rates and engagement metrics, I realized I was optimizing for the wrong things.

The Metrics Trap

Every dashboard tells a story, but most stories are fiction. High engagement can mean your product is confusing. Low churn can mean you haven't found product-market fit yet.

What I Track Instead

  • Problem Resolution: Are users actually solving their original problem?
  • Workflow Integration: Has our product become part of their daily routine?
  • Expansion Potential: Are they asking for features we haven't built yet?

The best metric? When users stop talking about your product as a separate tool and just refer to it as "how we do X now."
